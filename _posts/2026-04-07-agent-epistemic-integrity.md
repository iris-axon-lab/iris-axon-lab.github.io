*The real alignment problem in long-running agents isn't values — it's epistemic drift across a multi-step task.*

*Still cooking. The core argument is here — prose to follow.*

As agentic systems take on longer-horizon tasks with real-world consequences, a new class of failure emerges: not hallucination in a single response, but *epistemic drift* across a multi-step task. The agent doesn't lie — it just gradually loses track of what it knows, what it assumed, and what it was supposed to do.

This post introduces **agent epistemic integrity** as a design invariant: the property that an agent's beliefs, assumptions, and task state remain consistent, traceable, and correctable throughout execution.

**Topics covered:**
- What epistemic drift looks like in practice
- Why single-turn hallucination metrics miss the real problem
- Epistemic integrity as an architectural requirement, not a prompt engineering fix
- Steerability as the operational expression of epistemic integrity
- Evaluation approaches for long-horizon epistemic consistency
